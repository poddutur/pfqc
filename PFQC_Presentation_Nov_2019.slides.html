Ergodicity¶

  • Simple Question
    • You start with a total capital of 1000 dollars. You are offered a gamble on a fair coin toss, if it lands heads you pay 100 dollars and if it lands tails you win 110 dollars.
  • Assumptions
    • You are allowed to play this gamble multiple times.
    • You returns are always scaled by your capital. If your capital grows to 2000 dollars, you will pay 200 dollars on tails and win 220 dollars on heads.
  • How many of you would accept the gamble and why ?
  • Clearly it has a positive expected value, so one should be foolish not to accept !!? May not be true.
  • How does you capital change over time ?
  • The mean return from each round is 0.005 or 0.5%.
  • The variance of returns would be 0.011025. $$\partial{S_t} = \mu S_{t} \partial{dt} + \sigma S_{t} \partial {W_{t}}$$ $$\log\frac{S_{t}}{S_{0}} = (\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})t + \sigma \partial{W_{t}}$$
  • After time t, mean return is going to be $(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})*t$ and not $\mu*t$. We need to account for the risk in the trade.

  • $\mu*t$ can be considered as an ensemble average i.e average when things happen simultaneously, and $(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})*t$ can be considered as time average which in case of gamble the true expected value.
  • 100 people playing roulette at once is different from one person playing 100 rounds of roulette.
  • When these two are equal for a variable, then it is said to have the property of ergodicity.
  • Ergodicity: The expectation value of the observable is a constant (independent of time), and the finite-time average of the observable converges to this constant with probability one as the averaging time tends to infinity.
  • Reference: Evaluating gambles using dynamics by Ole Peters, Murray Gell-Mann

Jensen's inequality¶

  • Currently lets assume Dollar-Rupee exchange rate is 1.
  • In 6 months, dollar against Rupee has 50% probability of being 1.2 and 50% probability of being 0.8.
  • From a Dollar perspective
    • Expected Value : 0.5 x (1.2)+ 0.5 x (0.8) = 1
  • From a Rupee perspective
    • Expected Value : 0.5 x (1/1.2) + 0.5 x (1/0.8) = 1.042

But wait! shouldn't this be 1?

  • This follows from Jensen's inequality which $$E[f(x)] \neq f[E(x)]$$
  • You should be careful while changing the domain and comparing.
  • In reality when Dollar-Rupee increases by 20% to 1.2, Rupee-Dollar would decrease by 20% to 0.80 and not to (1/1.2) 0.83.
  • Reference: Solution to the Siegel Paradox KAM HON CHU

Convergence¶

  • At work we once ordered Baos from Wow Bao Restaurant for lunch.
  • Apart from the facilities team, none of the employees knew the number of Baos being ordered. We were around 400 employees. (So one could kind of guess.)
  • A slack channel started trying to predicted the number of baos ordered and soon a two sided liquid market followed.
  • At the end of the day actual number would be revealed.
  • I thought each would eat around 2.5 so they would probably ordered 1000.
  • At one point of the day the market was 2400 bid @ 2500 offer. So I sold.
  • After some point, the market was 1000 bid @ 1100 offer and I bought back for a profit.
  • Finally market was 1500 bid @ 1600 offered. But the actual number of orders was 1800. It seems there were 450 employees and they assumed 4 per each.
  • Market did not settle close to the actual value.
  • I made money even though I was fundamentally wrong.
  • This is not a very abstract example, in agriculture futures market there were instances when future did not converge to cash price at expiration.
  • Reference: Understanding the Non-Convergence of Agricultural Futures via Stochastic Storage Costs and Timing Options Kevin Guo Tim Leung
  • So how do you make money in the market ?
    • Do you need to be right on fundamentals ? OR
    • Do you need to be right on what the market thinks fundamentals are ?

Risk Vs Uncertainty¶

  • What is the different between risk and uncertainty ?
  • All outcomes are known and the probabilities of the outcomes are known. Its a rule based game.
  • All outcomes are known but the probabilities of the outcomes are not known.
  • Neither all outcomes are known nor the probabilities associated with them are known.
  • Statistics apply well in the first case and we can compute risk.
  • But in last two scenarios, we should be careful in applying mathematical tools.
  • Markets are complex adaptive systems.
  • Does dog catching a frisbee know newtonian mechanics ?
  • Heuristics work in 2-3 scenarios, coming up with good heuristics is a skill.
  • Reference: Simple Heuristics by Gerd Gigerenzer
